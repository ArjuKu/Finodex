## 1. The "Lemonade Stand" Metaphor
Imagine you want to buy a lemonade stand.
- If the stand makes **$100 profit per year**, and you pay **$1,000** for it, it will take you **10 years** to earn back your money.
- In the stock market, this is called the **P/E Ratio** (Price-to-Earnings).
- A researcher asks: "Is this stand worth $1,000, or is it really worth $800 because there's a new coffee shop opening nearby?"

**The Goal:** Find stocks that are **worth more than the price** the market is asking.

---

## 2. The Equity Research Dictionary
- **Intrinsic Value**: What the stock is *actually* worth (based on fundamentals), not what it's trading for today.
- **Market Price**: The current price on the stock exchange (driven by emotions, news, and supply/demand).
- **Margin of Safety**: Buying a stock for less than its intrinsic value. It's like buying a $100 bill for $70.
- **EPS (Earnings Per Share)**: The company's profit divided by every single share.
- **EBITDA**: "Profit before the boring stuff." (Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization).

---

## 3. Method 1: P/E Ratio
### The Concept
"How many years of profit does it take to pay back my investment?"

### Elementary Formula
```
P/E Ratio = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)
```

If Stock Price = $100 and EPS = $5, then P/E = 20x.
This means: "It takes 20 years of profits to pay back my $100."

### The Nuance
- **High P/E**: Investors expect high future growth (or the stock is overpriced!).
- **Low P/E**: The company is struggling (or it's a bargain!).

### Example
| Company | Stock Price | EPS | P/E | Interpretation |
|---------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Tech A | $50 | $2.00 | 25x | Expensive/Growth |
| Utility B | $40 | $4.00 | 10x | Cheap/Value |

## 4. Method 2: DCF (The "Time Machine" Formula)
### The Concept
A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. DCF brings future money back to today.

### Elementary Formula
```
Intrinsic Value = (Year 1 Cash ÷ 1.10) + (Year 2 Cash ÷ ) + ... + Terminal1.10² Value
```
*(Note: 1.10 is if your "required return" is 10%)*

### The Steps (Simplified)
1. Guess how much cash the company will make in the next 5 years.
2. "Discount" that cash back to today's dollars (because money loses value over time).
3. Add up all those dollars.
4. Ask: "Is this number higher than the current stock price?"

### The Nuance: Terminal Value
After 5 years, the company still exists! We estimate its "Terminal Value" using:
- **Gordon Growth**: Assume it grows forever at a small rate (e.g., 3%).
- **Exit Multiple**: Assume it sells for a standard multiple (e.g., 10x EBITDA).

---

## 5. Nuance: Sensitivity Analysis
The researcher doesn't just guess one price. They create a "What If" table:

| Stock Price | If Growth is 5% | If Growth is 7% | If Growth is 9% |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| P/E 15x | $40 | $50 | $60 |
| P/E 20x | $55 | $65 | $80 |

If the stock is trading at $55 but your analysis shows it's only worth $50 in the worst case, **don't buy it**.

---

## 6. Summary
Equity Research is about finding a **Margin of Safety**.
1. Use the P/E ratio to see how expensive the stock is.
2. Use DCF to find the "True Value" (Intrinsic Value).
3. Only buy if the Intrinsic Value is **significantly higher** than the Market Price.

**High Retention Recap:** Think of buying a stock like buying a lemonade stand. Calculate how many years of profit it takes to pay you back (P/E), estimate how much profit you'll make in the future and bring it to today's dollars (DCF), and only buy if you're getting a deal.
